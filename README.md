# Ergonomic Standing Geometry for Electric Bass and Guitar

## Defensive Publication

**Author:** Vincent Wong

**Purpose:** Public disclosure and defensive publication of an ergonomic design concept for electric bass guitars, electric guitars, and related fretted string instruments.

---

## Abstract

This repository documents an ergonomic instrument design concept developed to reduce the physical reach required by the fretting hand when playing while standing.

The concept is based on changing the geometric relationship between the instrument's support system (body and strap attachment points) and its playing geometry (nut, neck, scale length and bridge), while preserving the musical properties of the instrument.

Unlike conventional electric guitars and basses, where the body is positioned primarily according to historical and aesthetic conventions, this concept treats the body as a support structure whose position may be optimized independently from the instrument's scale geometry.

The repository contains photographs and documentation of multiple physical prototypes demonstrating the concept.

The publication is intended as prior art.

---

# Background

Electric guitars and basses have remained remarkably consistent in their overall geometry since their introduction.

Although there have been significant developments in electronics, materials, manufacturing methods, headless instruments, multi-scale instruments, body contours, lightweight construction and other ergonomic improvements, the fundamental relationship between the player's body and the instrument's playing geometry has remained largely unchanged.

When sitting, an electric guitar or bass naturally rests on the player's leg. This usually places the neck closer to the player's centreline, often rotates the neck upward, and reduces the reach required to play in first position.

When standing, the instrument instead hangs from a strap.

This changes the geometry between the player and the instrument.

In many conventional designs, the instrument hangs further towards the player's right side (for a right-handed player), increasing the distance between the player's left shoulder and the first frets.

This can require increased shoulder movement, greater arm extension, and less favourable wrist angles when playing in the lowest positions.

Players commonly compensate by:

- raising the strap,
- rotating the neck upward,
- changing posture,
- rotating the instrument,
- adapting technique,
- or accepting the ergonomic compromise.

The concept described here instead proposes changing the instrument geometry itself.

---

# The Design Principle

The fundamental idea is:

**Rather than adapting the player to traditional instrument geometry, adapt the instrument geometry to the player.**

The concept preserves:

- nut position relative to bridge,
- scale length,
- fret spacing,
- intonation,
- neck geometry,
- bridge function,
- normal playing technique.

Instead, the relationship between the instrument body and the scale geometry is modified.

The body, together with the suspension points used when the instrument is worn on a strap, is translated towards the nut relative to conventional body placement.

As a consequence, when the instrument hangs from a strap:

- the first frets are closer to the player's body,
- shoulder reach is reduced,
- left arm extension is reduced,
- first-position playing becomes more comfortable,
- the standing playing position becomes more similar to the seated playing position.

The musical geometry remains unchanged.

The ergonomic geometry changes.

# Prototype Development

The concept has been implemented in multiple working prototypes.

## Prototype 1 – Adjustable Body Position

The first prototype was designed as an experimental platform.

Instead of permanently fixing the body to the neck-through structure, the body could slide longitudinally along the neck-through beam using a rail system and locking mechanism.

This allowed the body, including the strap attachment points, to be repositioned relative to the fixed nut-to-bridge scale geometry.

The purpose of the adjustable prototype was to experimentally determine:

- optimal standing ergonomics,
- optimal first-position reach,
- balance,
- instrument suspension,
- player comfort,
- convenience of being able to adjust for different playing positions (like sitting or standing) - or for different player's requirements,
- practical body position.

This prototype demonstrated that the body position itself is an ergonomic design parameter rather than a fixed requirement.

---

## Prototype 2 – Fixed Optimized Geometry

A second instrument was built using a fixed body position derived from the experience gained with the adjustable prototype.

This demonstrated that the ergonomic principle could be implemented as a practical instrument rather than only as an experimental system.

# Generalisation of the Concept

The concept is intentionally broader than the specific instruments shown in this repository.

The essential idea is changing the spatial relationship between:

- the player's body,
- the suspension system,
- the instrument body,
- and the playing geometry,
depending on players position (sitting/standing etc) and/or different player's needs

Possible implementations include, but are not limited to:

- neck-through construction,
- bolt-on neck construction,
- set-neck construction,
- movable body structures,
- fixed translated body structures,
- adjustable suspension systems,
- alternative strap attachment locations,
- modular body systems,
- frame constructions,
- headless instruments,
- extended neck-through beams,
- or other mechanical implementations producing substantially the same ergonomic effect.

The implementation method is not the invention.

The ergonomic principle is.

# Scope

Although developed using electric bass guitars, the concept is applicable to:

- electric guitars,
- bass guitars,
- headless instruments,
- extended-range instruments,
- multi-scale instruments,
- travel instruments,
- custom instruments,
- experimental instruments,
- and other fretted string instruments.
